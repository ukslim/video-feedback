In the 90s I would point a video camera at a screen showing its own view, hold a flame between them, and watch it feed back into swirling patterns.

Create a simulation of this, filling the browser pane. The "flame" is a blob that pulsates gently and chaotically in size and hue, between red, orange, yellow and white. It starts at the centre of the pane and is affected by camera movement.

On all platforms we can move the "camera" by dragging in sectors of the pane:

- top third: up/down/left/right
- middle third: pitch/yaw
- bottom third: closer/further from "screen"

What should emerge (without explicit programming) is that moving closer causes the trail to whoosh towards the viewer, moving further back causes the trail to recede into the distance. Yaw causes the trail to spin. And so on.

On platforms where a gyroscope is available, using it should give the user the sensation of pointing a real camera at a real screen; dragging still works for correction.

Simulate a system that automatically tunes brightness and contrast so that the image doesn't feed back into an empty white or black screen; we should see the pattern of swirls.
